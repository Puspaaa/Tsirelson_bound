The Correlation Landscape

Where does Quantum Mechanics sit?

Local (\(\mathcal{L}\))
\(S \leq 2\) — Classical Physics
Quantum (\(\mathcal{Q}\))
\(S \leq 2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.83\) — Tsirelson's Bound
No-Signaling (\(\mathcal{NS}\))
\(S \leq 4\) — Relativity Limit
The Gap:  \(\mathcal{L} \subset \mathcal{Q} \subset \mathcal{NS}\)

Why does nature leave space between \(\mathcal{Q}\) and \(\mathcal{NS}\) empty?

We visualize the space of all possible theories as nested shapes.

Inner shape — classical physics. Only correlations obeying Bell inequalities.
Outer square — the No-Signaling limit, everything allowed by Special Relativity.
Quantum Mechanics lives in between, in this convex body bounded by \(S = 2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.83\).

The question is: Why can't we reach the corners of the No-Signaling square? Why is there a gap?

The CHSH Score

Quantifying non-locality

\[ S \;=\; \sum_{x,y} P(a \oplus b = x \cdot y \mid x,y) \]
  • Classical: Limited by Logic  \(S \leq 2\)
  • Quantum: Limited by Tsirelson's Bound  \(S \leq 2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.83\)
  • No-Signaling: Algebraically allows  \(S \leq 4\)

Tsirelson (1980) proved that no quantum state can exceed \(2\sqrt{2}\). But why?

The CHSH quantity S measures how well two parties can win a specific correlation game. Classical physics gives at most S = 2. Quantum mechanics reaches \(2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.83\). But algebraically, S can go all the way to 4 without violating no-signaling. Tsirelson proved the quantum bound in 1980, but the question "Why this value?" remained open for decades.

The Playground: General Probabilistic Theories

Beyond Hilbert spaces — an operational framework

❌ Traditional QM

States as vectors \(\psi \in \mathcal{H}\) in Hilbert Spaces

Too specific — assumes the answer!

✓ Operational View

Definition of a State \(\vec{P}\):
A list of probabilities for \(k\) fiducial measurements.

\(\vec{P} = \big( P(i|j) \big)_{i,j}\)

Outcome \(i\) given measurement \(j\)

  • Convexity: Mixtures of states are valid states
  • Transformations: \(\vec{P} \mapsto M \cdot \vec{P}\) (linear maps)
  • Measurements: Effects \(\vec{R}_i\) with \(\vec{R}_i \cdot \vec{P} = P(\text{outcome } i)\)
To ask "Why is QM the way it is?", we can't use QM itself as a framework — that would be circular. Instead, we work in the framework of General Probabilistic Theories (GPTs).

A state is simply defined by the probabilities it assigns to fiducial measurements. We don't assume Hilbert spaces. The set of states must be convex (mixtures are allowed), and transformations are linear maps on these probability vectors.

Quantum theory, classical probability theory, and exotic theories like "box-world" all fit into this framework.

Defining Reality: Systems of Type \((k, l)\)

What distinguishes Quantum from Box-World?

System Type \((k, l)\):  \(k\) measurements, \(l\) outcomes each.

Quantum

  • Continuous measurements
  • \(k \approx d^2\) fiducial measurements
  • State space: Bloch sphere

Box-World

  • Elementary system: type \((2,2)\)
  • \(k = 2\) fiducial measurements
  • State space: square (all no-signaling distributions)

PR-Box: Lives in box-world. Defined by the marginal probabilities of just 2 measurements on a \((2,2)\) system.

In GPTs, we classify systems by their "type" \((k, l)\): the number of fiducial measurements \(k\) and outcomes per measurement \(l\).

Quantum theory for a qubit has \(k = d^2 = 4\), with a continuous set of measurements and the Bloch sphere as state space.

Box-world is the theory where elementary systems have type \((2,2)\) and the state space includes all no-signaling distributions. Its state space is a square — larger than the Bloch disk. The PR-box lives at its corners.

The Rules: Composite Systems & No-Signaling

How systems combine — and what they cannot do

1. Composite Systems

If Alice has space \(V_A\) and Bob has \(V_B\):

\(V_{AB} = V_A \otimes V_B\)

Joint state defined by \(P(ii'|jj')\).

2. No-Signaling Principle

Marginals are independent of remote settings:

\(P_A(i|j) = \sum_{i'} P_{AB}(ii'|jj')\)

Measuring B cannot reveal what transformation was applied to A.

Key Point: Box-world satisfies all these rules... yet it violates Tsirelson's bound.

In the GPT framework, composite systems are built via the tensor product \(V_{AB} = V_A \otimes V_B\). The joint state is fully specified by probabilities \(P(ii'|jj')\).

No-signaling follows automatically from the assumption that operations on A and B can be applied in any order. It means Alice's marginal statistics don't depend on what Bob does.

The critical point: Box-world satisfies all these structural rules — convexity, tensor products, no-signaling — yet its correlations can violate Tsirelson's bound. So we need an additional principle to recover the quantum limit. That principle is the Data Processing Inequality.

The Suspect: PR-Boxes

Popescu & Rohrlich (1994)

Definition

A hypothetical device with inputs \(x, y \in \{0,1\}\) and outputs \(a, b \in \{0,1\}\) such that:

  • \(P(a = b \mid 00) = 1\) — perfect correlation
  • \(P(a = b \mid 01) = 1\)
  • \(P(a = b \mid 10) = 1\)
  • \(P(a \neq b \mid 11) = 1\) — anti-correlation
  • CHSH Score:  \(S = 4\) (maximal!)
  • Respects No-Signaling — marginals are uniform
  • Violates Tsirelson's Bound

It doesn't violate relativity. So why doesn't nature allow it?

At the corner of the No-Signaling square lives the "PR-Box", proposed by Popescu and Rohrlich in 1994.

It is designed to win the CHSH game 100% of the time (\(S=4\)). Its local marginals are uniformly random — so it cannot be used to send signals faster than light. Strictly speaking, it is a valid physical theory from the No-Signaling perspective.

But we don't see it in our universe. We need a new principle — beyond just no-signaling — to rule it out.

The Deeper Suspect: Data Processing Inequality

Correlations cannot be created locally

The Claim

If a theory respects DPI, it respects Tsirelson's Bound.

Data Processing Inequality (DPI)

For any state \(\vec{P}_{AB}\) and any local transformation \(T: B \to B'\):

\(H(A|B)_{\vec{P}_{AB}} \;\leq\; H(A|B')_{(\mathbb{1} \otimes T)\vec{P}_{AB}}\)

Local operations on B cannot increase the correlations between A and B.

  • \(H(A|B)\) = conditional entropy = \(H(AB) - H(B)\)  (COND)
  • \(H\) reduces to Shannon entropy for classical systems  (SHAN)
  • DPI + COND + SHAN  \(\Rightarrow\)  Tsirelson's Bound
Here is the central claim of the paper: if any generalized probabilistic theory respects the Data Processing Inequality, then it automatically respects Tsirelson's bound.

DPI says: applying a local operation T on Bob's system cannot increase the correlations between Alice and Bob, as measured by conditional entropy.

The proof uses three ingredients: DPI itself, the definition of conditional entropy (COND), and the fact that entropy reduces to Shannon entropy for classical systems (SHAN). These three conditions together imply information causality, which in turn implies Tsirelson's bound.

Box-world violates DPI (and strong subadditivity), which is precisely why it can exceed the quantum limit.

The Goal: Bob wants to guess \(a_b\).

Efficiency: \(\eta = P(\beta = a_b)\)

Information Causality states:  \(\displaystyle\sum_i I(a_i : \beta \mid b=i) \;\leq\; m\)

The total information Bob extracts about Alice's bits cannot exceed what she sent.

To convict the PR-box, we don't measure energy or momentum. We measure Information Capacity.

We play a game: Alice has 2 random bits. She can only send 1 classical bit to Bob. Bob must guess one of Alice's bits, chosen at random.

In a classical world, Bob can't do better than guessing one bit perfectly and the other at random. But what if they share a PR-box?

Information Causality says: the total mutual information Bob can extract is bounded by the number of bits Alice sent.
